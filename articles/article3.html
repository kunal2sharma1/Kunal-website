Centralisation vs. Federalism: India's Governance Fault Lines

Federalism Public Policy Governance India

The Indian Constitution does not describe India as a federation. It calls India a "Union of States" — a deliberate formulation chosen by the framers to signal that, unlike the American model, India's constituent units were not indestructible entities that had surrendered powers to a central authority. The Union could be reorganised; states could be created, altered, or abolished. Power, in the final analysis, flowed downward from the Constitution, not upward from the states.

This constitutional architecture has always generated tension. India is, by almost any sociological measure, one of the most diverse polities in the world — linguistically, religiously, ecologically, and economically. Governing such diversity through a unitary framework would be neither feasible nor legitimate. Yet the Centre retains enormous constitutional and political tools to shape the exercise of state authority. The result is what constitutional scholar K.C. Wheare famously called a "quasi-federal" system — federal in form but unitary in tendency.

In recent years, these tensions have sharpened. This essay examines three specific fault lines: fiscal federalism and the structure of revenue sharing, the use of Governor's office as a political instrument, and the politics of the Concurrent List.

The Constitutional Design: Centralist by Intent

India's constitutional drafters were profoundly influenced by the trauma of Partition, by concerns about national integration, and by the administrative model they inherited from the British. B.R. Ambedkar, presenting the draft Constitution, described it as "a dual polity" in which the Centre was deliberately given overriding authority in specified domains.

The Seventh Schedule distributes legislative authority across three lists: the Union List (97 subjects exclusively within Parliamentary jurisdiction), the State List (66 subjects for state legislatures), and the Concurrent List (47 subjects on which both can legislate, with Union law prevailing in case of conflict). Article 356 — the President's Rule provision — allows the Union to dissolve state governments and assume direct central control, a power that has been invoked over 100 times since independence.

This design was not accidental. The framers believed that a strong Centre was necessary to hold together a newly-independent, partition-scarred, economically fragile nation. Whether that original rationale still justifies the degree of centralisation is a question that subsequent generations have continued to contest.

Fiscal Federalism: Who Controls the Purse?

Fiscal federalism — the distribution of revenue-raising authority and expenditure responsibilities across levels of government — is perhaps the most consequential dimension of federal relations. In India, the Finance Commission, constituted every five years, determines the formula for sharing central tax revenues with states.

The Goods and Services Tax (GST), introduced in 2017, represented both a significant step toward economic integration and a controversial restructuring of fiscal federalism. By subsuming a wide range of state taxes into a centralised collection mechanism governed by the GST Council, it reduced states' independent revenue-raising capacity. The Council's voting structure — where the Centre holds one-third of votes and decisions require a three-fourths majority — means that the Union's concurrence is essential for any change, while a bloc of state governments can be overridden.

Southern states — particularly Kerala, Tamil Nadu, and Karnataka — have been among the most vocal critics of the current fiscal framework. Their argument is structural: states with better economic performance, lower fertility rates, and higher tax compliance effectively subsidise redistribution to states with different profiles, while retaining insufficient fiscal autonomy to manage their own developmental priorities. The 15th Finance Commission's decision to use 2011 census data for population-based allocation — rather than the 1971 data that had previously been used — was seen by southern states as penalising them for successful family planning.

The Governor's Office as Political Instrument

The Governor of each Indian state is appointed by the President on the advice of the Union government. Constitutionally, the Governor is a nominal head — bound to act on the advice of the elected Council of Ministers. In practice, the Governor's office has frequently been used as a political instrument by the Centre against state governments controlled by opposition parties.

Recent years have produced a string of constitutional controversies around Gubernatorial conduct. In Maharashtra, Kerala, Tamil Nadu, Telangana, and West Bengal, Governors have delayed assenting to Bills passed by state legislatures, refused to convene Assembly sessions, made public statements critical of elected state governments, and in some cases, reportedly withheld action on budget proposals. The Supreme Court has been drawn into several of these disputes, and in 2023 ruled — in a case concerning the Punjab Governor — that Governors cannot indefinitely withhold assent to Bills or sit on summons for Assembly sessions.

"The Governor is not an agent of the Union government. He is a constitutional functionary with defined duties and defined limits. The Constitution does not contemplate the Governor as a mechanism of political control over elected state governments." — Supreme Court, State of Punjab v. Principal Secretary to the Governor of Punjab (2023)

The Concurrent List and Legislative Conflict

The Concurrent List covers domains including education, criminal law, forests, economic and social planning, and — significantly — labour relations. Parliamentary legislation on Concurrent List subjects overrides state legislation in case of conflict, making the management of this list a persistent source of federal tension.

The farm laws passed by Parliament in 2020 — subsequently repealed following sustained farmer protests — illustrated this tension sharply. Agriculture is a State List subject, but the Union government argued that its legislation was anchored in trade and commerce provisions. Several state governments, including Punjab, passed their own counter-legislation. The episode exposed the contested boundary between Union and State competence and the political consequences of legislating centrally on subjects with deep state-level stakeholders.

Similarly, the National Education Policy and the proposed All India Judicial Services — which would centralise judicial recruitment currently managed by state Public Service Commissions — have been resisted by state governments as encroachments on domains they consider appropriately within state jurisdiction.

Cooperative Federalism vs. Competitive Federalism

The official discourse of the current Union government has emphasised "cooperative federalism" — a model in which Centre and states work together toward shared national goals. The NITI Aayog, which replaced the Planning Commission in 2015, was designed partly with this model in mind, giving Chief Ministers formal seats in a national policy body.

Critics argue that cooperative federalism, in practice, has often meant states cooperating with centrally-determined priorities rather than genuinely shared deliberation over national direction. The implementation of centrally-sponsored schemes — which now account for a significant portion of Union transfers to states — requires states to follow Union guidelines, contribute matching funds, and align their policies with central parameters, even in domains that are constitutionally within State jurisdiction.

The alternative concept of "competitive federalism" — where states compete to attract investment and talent by improving governance, infrastructure, and ease of doing business — captures a different dimension of the federal dynamic. India's states do indeed compete in these ways, and this competition has produced genuine developmental achievements. But competition in contexts of unequal fiscal capacity and centralised regulatory authority inevitably advantages states that are already better-positioned.

Conclusion: Federalism as Ongoing Negotiation

India's federal arrangement has never been a settled constitutional settlement — it has always been a continuing negotiation between the Centre and states over the terms of governance in a diverse polity. The tensions discussed in this essay — over fiscal resources, gubernatorial conduct, and legislative competence — are not aberrations. They are structural features of a constitutional design that concentrated power at the Centre while promising meaningful subnational governance.

What has changed in recent years is the intensity and the visibility of these tensions. State governments with different political complexions from the Centre are more assertive in articulating their constitutional rights; the Supreme Court has taken a more active role in policing constitutional boundaries; and civil society in several states has mobilised around federal grievances with renewed energy.

Whether India's federal balance shifts — toward genuine decentralisation or toward further consolidation at the Centre — will depend substantially on the political choices made by actors at both levels of government, and on the willingness of constitutional institutions to enforce the limits that the framers, despite their centralist instincts, embedded in the constitutional text.
