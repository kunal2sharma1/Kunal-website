Is Indian Democracy Weakening? An Institutional Analysis

Democracy Governance India Institutions

India's democratic identity has long been considered one of its most remarkable achievements. Gaining independence in 1947, it chose universal adult franchise at a time when much of the developing world — and significant portions of the developed — had not. For decades, Indian democracy was studied as an anomaly: a poor, multilingual, multi-religious nation that somehow maintained competitive elections, judicial review, and a free press. That consensus is now under challenge.

Over the past decade, a growing body of political scientists, constitutional scholars, and democracy monitoring organisations have begun asking a troubling question: is India experiencing democratic backsliding? This essay examines that question through an institutional lens — focusing not on electoral outcomes, which remain competitive, but on the deeper structural conditions that sustain or erode democratic governance.

Defining Democratic Backsliding

Democratic backsliding, as theorised by scholars such as Steven Levitsky and Lucan Way, refers to the incremental deterioration of democratic norms and institutions, typically by elected governments rather than through outright coups. Unlike the authoritarian reversals of the twentieth century, contemporary backsliding is often gradual, procedurally legitimate, and difficult to pin to any single act.

Key indicators include: erosion of judicial independence, restrictions on press freedom, weakening of electoral commissions and anti-corruption bodies, politicisation of the civil service, and the narrowing of space for civil society and opposition. India displays concerning trajectories on several of these dimensions simultaneously.

"Backsliding is not the death of democracy — it is democracy's slow suffocation. It is the gradual loss of the habits, norms, and institutional protections that make elections meaningful."

The Judiciary Under Strain

The Indian judiciary, particularly the Supreme Court, has historically been a check on executive overreach. From the landmark Kesavananda Bharati judgment of 1973 — which established the basic structure doctrine — to its role during the Emergency, the Court has periodically asserted constitutional limits on state power.

In recent years, however, critics have noted a pattern of institutional deference. Petitions challenging the abrogation of Article 370 in Jammu & Kashmir, the Citizenship Amendment Act, and electoral bond schemes languished for years before hearings were scheduled. The process by which judges are elevated to the Supreme Court — the collegium system — has itself come under executive pressure, with documented instances of recommended names being returned or delayed.

The Supreme Court's 2024 ruling on electoral bonds, which declared the scheme unconstitutional, was welcomed as a reassertion of judicial authority. But constitutional scholars note that the scheme had operated for several years and that the delay itself had consequences for democratic transparency. Judicial independence is measured not only by final verdicts but by the willingness to act in time.

Press Freedom and the Mediascape

India's performance on press freedom indices has declined markedly. In Reporters Without Borders' 2023 World Press Freedom Index, India ranked 161st out of 180 countries — a position that places it below several nations with significantly less established democratic traditions. The use of sedition laws (though the Supreme Court put them in abeyance in 2022), the Foreign Contribution (Regulation) Act against critical media organisations, and income tax raids on newsrooms have all been documented as mechanisms of indirect pressure.

The ownership structure of Indian media has undergone significant transformation. Conglomerate ownership — where major business groups with significant government interests own news channels — creates structural incentives for self-censorship that are more durable than any formal censorship regime. The result is not a silenced press but a systematically deferential one.

Electoral Institutions and Their Credibility

Free and fair elections remain central to Indian democracy. Voter turnout is high, constituencies are genuinely contested, and opposition parties do win at both state and national levels. However, the institutional infrastructure surrounding elections has faced scrutiny.

The Election Commission of India (ECI), once considered a model of administrative independence, has seen questions raised about its composition and conduct. The Supreme Court's 2023 ruling that the appointment process for Election Commissioners must be reformed — removing the executive's exclusive role — reflected a judicial acknowledgement that the existing arrangement was constitutionally problematic. The Model Code of Conduct, which restricts government announcements during election periods, has been criticised for inconsistent enforcement.

Civil Society and Dissent

A healthy democracy requires robust civil society — the networks of associations, movements, and organisations that operate between the individual and the state. In India, civil society has historically been vibrant, encompassing everything from trade unions to environmental NGOs to human rights organisations.

Recent years have seen significant contraction in this space. The FCRA (Foreign Contribution Regulation Act) has been used to cancel the licences of thousands of NGOs, including Amnesty International India and the Ford Foundation's Indian grantees. Activists and academics have faced arrest under laws including the Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act (UAPA), which permits extended detention without bail. The Bhima Koregaon case — involving prominent academics and activists — has been widely cited by human rights organisations as emblematic of these trends.

Comparative Perspective: Where Does India Stand?

Global democracy indices offer a useful external benchmark. Freedom House downgraded India from "Free" to "Partly Free" in 2021, citing "a multiyear democratic decline." V-Dem's Liberal Democracy Index shows India's scores declining from the mid-2000s onward, with sharper declines after 2014. The Economist Intelligence Unit's Democracy Index classifies India as a "flawed democracy," down from a higher ranking in earlier editions.

These assessments are contested by the Indian government and by scholars who argue that external indices reflect Western biases and fail to capture the genuine democratic participation of India's vast electorate. That critique has merit — no index is neutral, and India's mass electoral participation is a democratic achievement. But the specific institutional concerns raised by these organisations are not easily dismissed on methodological grounds.

Conclusion: Weakening Without Dying

Indian democracy is not collapsing. Elections are held, results are accepted, opposition parties govern states, courts occasionally push back, and civil society persists despite constraint. What is occurring is more subtle and more dangerous: a hollowing out of the institutional substance of democratic governance while its electoral form is maintained.

This distinction matters enormously. Competitive elections in conditions of weakened judicial independence, constrained press, and shrinking civil society space produce different political outcomes than elections embedded within genuinely pluralist institutions. The question for India is not whether it will hold elections — it will — but whether those elections take place in conditions that make meaningful democratic choice possible.

The answer, on present evidence, is: less so than before. Whether that trajectory can be reversed depends substantially on whether citizens, institutions, and political forces within India identify it as a problem requiring urgent collective response.
