The Role of Media in Constructing Political Power

Media Political Theory Power Democracy

The relationship between media and political power is among the most consequential in modern democratic societies, and among the most misunderstood. In popular discourse, media is imagined either as a watchdog — an independent fourth estate holding power to account — or as a propaganda arm of the state. Both images capture something real, but neither captures the full complexity of how media actually functions as a terrain where power is produced, contested, and reproduced.

This essay draws on three theoretical traditions — Antonio Gramsci's concept of hegemony, Noam Chomsky and Edward Herman's propaganda model, and Jürgen Habermas's theory of the public sphere — to develop a more nuanced account of how media constructs and sustains political power. It then applies this framework to the contemporary Indian media environment.

Gramsci and the Manufacture of Consent

Writing from a Fascist prison in 1930s Italy, Antonio Gramsci developed his concept of hegemony to explain why subordinated groups often accept the ideological frameworks of dominant classes without coercion. Hegemony operates not through force but through the production of a common sense — a set of assumptions about the natural order of things — that makes existing power relations appear inevitable, rational, and even desirable.

Media institutions are central to this hegemonic function. By consistently representing certain political positions as reasonable and others as extreme, by setting the parameters of legitimate debate, and by naturalising the interests of particular social groups as universal interests, media organisations do ideological work. This work is most effective precisely when it is invisible — when it appears not as the exercise of power but as the neutral reporting of facts.

"The press is not only a collective propagandist and a collective agitator, it is also a collective organiser." — Gramsci's reformulation, applied to mass media in democratic contexts.

Chomsky and Herman: The Propaganda Model

Herman and Chomsky's 1988 work Manufacturing Consent offered a structural analysis of American mainstream media that complemented Gramsci's cultural theory. Their propaganda model identified five "filters" through which news is processed before reaching the public: media ownership and profit orientation; advertising as primary revenue source; sourcing (reliance on official and institutional sources); flak (organised criticism of non-compliant reporting); and anti-communist ideology (later updated to include the "war on terror").

The model does not require a conspiracy. Media organisations staffed by conscientious journalists can consistently produce deferential coverage simply by internalising the structural constraints they operate within. Journalists who challenge these constraints face editorial resistance, marginalisation, and career consequences — not necessarily through explicit censorship but through the routine application of professional norms that serve to reproduce existing power relations.

Applied to India, several of these filters operate with particular force. The consolidation of media ownership within a small number of large conglomerates — including groups with significant interests in sectors regulated by the government — creates structural dependencies that constrain editorial independence. Advertising revenue in a market where the government is one of the largest advertisers creates additional incentives for deference.

Habermas and the Public Sphere

Jürgen Habermas's concept of the public sphere offers a normative counterpoint to the critical tradition of Gramsci and Chomsky. For Habermas, the public sphere designates a domain of social life in which private individuals come together to form a deliberating public — communicating by means of reason about matters of general concern, with a view to shaping public opinion that can hold state power to account.

Habermas traced the historical emergence of this sphere in eighteenth-century Europe — in coffee houses, literary salons, and a nascent free press — and its subsequent "refeudalization" through the twentieth-century commercialisation of mass media. As media became dominated by large corporations oriented toward profit rather than deliberation, the quality of public discourse deteriorated: spectacle replaced argument, publicity replaced reason, and opinion management replaced genuine democratic deliberation.

The digital transformation of media presents both opportunities and threats to this normative ideal. Social media platforms have dramatically lowered the barriers to entry for political speech, enabling voices previously excluded from mainstream discourse to participate. But they have simultaneously enabled the spread of disinformation, the algorithmic amplification of outrage, and the fragmentation of shared informational environments that Habermas considered prerequisites for genuine deliberation.

Indian Television News: A Case Study

Indian television news — particularly the Hindi-language channels that command the largest audiences — presents a striking illustration of these theoretical frameworks in action. The format of primetime debate shows, which became dominant in the 2010s, does not so much facilitate deliberation as perform its absence: high-decibel arguments, simultaneous speaking, constant interruptions, and theatrical confrontations between panellists produce heat without light.

More significantly, the agenda-setting function of these channels consistently prioritises certain narratives over others. Coverage patterns that amplify security threats, emphasise communal tensions, and focus on personality politics over policy analysis serve particular ideological functions, regardless of whether they reflect editorial intent. Studies of prime-time news coverage in India have documented significant disparities in how the same events are covered depending on which political actors are involved.

Digital Media: Disruption and Reconfiguration

The rise of digital media has not simply democratised political communication — it has reconfigured the terrain of power in more complex ways. WhatsApp has become a primary vector for political mobilisation in India, including the dissemination of disinformation. The architecture of platforms optimised for engagement — which tends to reward emotionally provocative content over accurate but mundane reporting — has created informational ecosystems in which falsehoods spread faster than corrections.

At the same time, independent digital news outlets have emerged as significant sources of accountability journalism that mainstream broadcasters increasingly decline to pursue. Organisations such as The Wire, Scroll, and The Quint have published investigations into electoral bond transactions, police misconduct, and governmental data manipulation that have entered the public record despite concerted efforts at suppression.

Conclusion: Media as Political Battleground

Media is not a neutral transmission mechanism through which political reality passes unchanged into public consciousness. It is an active producer of that reality — a terrain on which the meaning of political events is constructed, contested, and naturalised. The frameworks of Gramsci, Chomsky, and Habermas each illuminate different dimensions of this process: the cultural production of hegemony, the structural filters of institutional deference, and the normative ideal of democratic deliberation against which actual practice falls short.

For democratic citizens, understanding these dynamics is not an academic luxury. It is a prerequisite for meaningful participation in a political world that is partly constituted by the media through which it is perceived. Cultivating media literacy — the capacity to read news not simply as information but as the product of institutional, economic, and ideological processes — is among the most important democratic competences of the contemporary era.
